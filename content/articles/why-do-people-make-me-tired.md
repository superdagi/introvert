# Why Do People Make Me Tired?

Some people come home from a social day feeling energized and ready for more.

Others come home feeling completely drained.

If you recognize yourself in the latter, you're not alone.

## The Mental Work of Social Interaction

When we're with other people, our brain is constantly working. It interprets facial expressions, body language, tone of voice, and social dynamics. At the same time, we're trying to formulate our own thoughts and respond appropriately in conversation.

For many people, this happens completely automatically. But for people who are more sensitive to social stimulation, this can use a lot of mental energy.

**This doesn't mean you don't like people.** It just means your brain uses more energy on social processing.

## The Power of Solitude

This is why alone time can function as a kind of recharge. When stimulation is reduced, the brain gets a chance to recover.

Think of it like this:
- **Extroverted brains** often gain energy from external stimulation
- **Introverted brains** often need quiet time to process and recharge

Both are completely normal ways of functioning. Understanding your own pattern can help you manage your energy better and plan your social activities in a way that works for you.

## Finding Your Balance

The key is finding the right balance between social time and solitude. This might mean:

- Building buffer time before and after social events
- Choosing smaller gatherings over large parties
- Taking breaks during long social days
- Not feeling guilty about declining invitations when you need rest

Remember: Taking care of your social energy isn't antisocial—it's self-care.
